Where Languages Go Silent

Eight map pages from UNESCO's Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger (2010) — every dot a language losing its speakers. The same dots read two ways: a linguist's census, and what the quiet means in the places it falls.

Source: UNESCO, Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger, 3rd edition (2010), ed. Christopher Moseley — digitized at the Internet Archive. CC BY-SA 4.0.

North America

Linguist's reading

**Haida** (X̱aad Kíl) — a language isolate of Haida Gwaii, British Columbia. The 2010 atlas marks it *critically endangered*: fewer than 50 fluent speakers, nearly all elders. Two dialects, Massett and Skidegate, each with its own orthography.

Community reading

On Haida Gwaii the count of fluent elders is told person by person, name by name. Master–apprentice pairs now sit teenagers beside grandmothers for hours of Haida-only conversation — the dot on this map is also a kitchen table.

Every dot on these pages is a language; its colour is how close the talking is to stopping. Switch between the **Linguist's** and **Community** readings to hear the same dots two ways — or stay on *Base* to see only this note.

Western South America

The Andes–Amazon corridor is the densest stretch of dots in the whole atlas — dozens of small languages between the mountains and the river basin.

Linguist's reading

**Chamicuro** — Arawakan, spoken at Pampa Hermosa on the Huallaga river, Peru. The atlas lists it *critically endangered*: by 2010, perhaps two elderly speakers remained, the rest of the community having shifted to Spanish.

Community reading

A dictionary of Chamicuro exists because its last speakers wanted one — 'so the grandchildren can see what we sounded like.' The dot here is an act of deliberate remembering, not just a casualty count.

The Caucasus

The Caucasus packs more language families into one mountain range than most continents hold — and more red dots.

Linguist's reading

**Ubykh** — Northwest Caucasian, famed among linguists for its consonant inventory of around eighty against just two distinctive vowels. The atlas marks it *extinct*: the last fully competent speaker, Tevfik Esenç, died in 1992 in Turkey.

Community reading

Ubykh did not fade in place — it was carried out of the Caucasus in the deportations of 1864 and went silent in exile, a century and a half later and a thousand kilometres from Sochi. Some silences on this map begin with an army.

Western Africa

On the West African coast, languages rarely die into silence — they dissolve into bigger neighbours, market tongue by market tongue.

Linguist's reading

**Mani** (Bullom So) — a Mel language of the Sierra Leone–Guinea coast, marked *critically endangered*. Documentation in the 2000s found most remaining speakers elderly, with daily life conducted in Soso and Temne.

Community reading

Mani didn't lose its speakers — it lost its occasions. Markets, weddings, and prayers moved into bigger neighbouring languages one domain at a time, until Mani was what grandparents used when they didn't want to be overheard.

Siberia

Across Siberia the dots trace the rivers — each one a small language of herders or fishers ringed by Russian.

Linguist's reading

**Tundra Yukaghir** — one of two survivors of the once-widespread Yukaghir family, lower Kolyma, Sakha Republic. The atlas counts speakers in the tens, marked *severely endangered*; nearly all are of the oldest generation.

Community reading

Transmission broke in the boarding-school decades, when children spent ten months a year away from the tundra and came home preferring Russian. The elders who herd reindeer in Yukaghir now teach it as a school subject — an hour a week.

The Himalaya

Every Himalayan valley is its own speech island; the atlas marks the passes where the islands are sinking.

Linguist's reading

**Dura** — Tibeto-Burman, Lamjung district, Nepal. The atlas marks it *extinct*: Soma Devi Dura, regarded as its last fluent speaker, died in 2008. What survives was recorded from her and from partial rememberers in the final years.

Community reading

The Dura community still exists — it is the language that went. Identity outlived speech: people who never learned Dura still gather its word-lists, because the name on the map is their own.

South-East Asia

Mainland South-East Asia layers its languages by altitude — the endangered ones live uphill, away from the lowland national tongues.

Linguist's reading

**Arem** — Vietic (Austroasiatic), spoken on the Vietnam–Laos border by a few dozen people at most in 2010. Of interest to linguists for preserving sesquisyllabic word shapes that Vietnamese itself has lost.

Community reading

The Arem were resettled out of the limestone forests into mixed villages; the language of the caves and foraging routes had no work to do in the new economy. Two generations later, it survives mainly in what elders remember of the forest.

Australia

Australia holds some of the world’s steepest language loss — and, in places like Broome, some of its most determined turnarounds.

Linguist's reading

**Yawuru** — Nyulnyulan family, the language of the Broome region, Western Australia. The 2010 atlas marks it *critically endangered*, with only a handful of elderly fluent speakers remaining.

Community reading

Broome answered the count another way: Yawuru became a compulsory subject in the town's schools, taught by the community itself. The dot marks both a near-silence and the town that decided not to let it finish.
